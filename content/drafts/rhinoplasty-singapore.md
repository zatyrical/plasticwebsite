# Rhinoplasty in Singapore

Rhinoplasty is surgery to reshape or reconstruct the nose. It may be performed for aesthetic reasons, functional breathing concerns, post-traumatic deformity, congenital nasal differences, previous surgery, or a combination of these issues.

In Asian rhinoplasty, planning often involves careful assessment of the nasal bridge, tip support, skin thickness, nostril shape, facial proportions and the patient’s preferred degree of change. The aim is not to create a standard nose shape. A well-planned rhinoplasty should respect the patient’s facial features, ethnic anatomy, breathing function and personal goals.

## What is rhinoplasty?

Rhinoplasty may involve changing the bone, cartilage, soft tissue or skin envelope of the nose. Depending on the patient’s concerns, surgery may address:

- nasal bridge height or contour
- nasal tip shape, projection or definition
- nasal width or asymmetry
- nostril shape or alar flare
- deviation after trauma or previous surgery
- breathing obstruction related to nasal structure
- reconstruction after injury, tumour removal or congenital conditions
- revision after previous rhinoplasty

Rhinoplasty is highly individual. Small changes can affect the overall balance of the face, and both appearance and breathing function need to be considered during planning.

## Who may consider rhinoplasty?

Rhinoplasty may be considered by patients who are concerned about:

- a low, flat, high, irregular or deviated nasal bridge
- a broad, bulbous, drooping or under-supported nasal tip
- nostril asymmetry or excessive alar flare
- a nose that appears out of proportion with the rest of the face
- post-traumatic nasal deformity
- breathing concerns related to nasal structure
- previous rhinoplasty results that require reassessment

Some patients seek rhinoplasty mainly for aesthetic refinement. Others have functional or reconstructive concerns. A consultation is needed to distinguish these issues because the surgical plan and expectations may differ.

## Asian rhinoplasty considerations

Asian rhinoplasty often requires a different planning approach from rhinoplasty in patients with naturally high nasal bridges or thinner nasal skin. Common considerations include thicker skin, lower bridge height, weaker tip support, shorter nasal length, nostril shape and the balance between augmentation and refinement.

The goal is usually to create a nose that fits the patient’s face rather than a nose that looks surgically overdone or borrowed from another person. Planning should include discussion of how much change is desired, how natural or defined the result should appear, and what trade-offs may be involved.

## Nasal bridge and tip refinement

Rhinoplasty may involve refinement of the nasal bridge, nasal tip, or both.

Bridge surgery may aim to improve height, smooth contour irregularities, adjust deviation or create a more balanced profile. Tip surgery may involve reshaping or supporting the tip cartilages to improve projection, rotation, definition or symmetry.

In many Asian patients, the bridge and tip need to be assessed together. Increasing bridge height without considering tip support can create imbalance. Similarly, tip refinement may be limited by skin thickness, cartilage strength and the existing nasal framework.

## Implant, cartilage and rib graft options

Rhinoplasty may use implants, the patient’s own cartilage, or a combination of materials depending on the indication and anatomy.

Autologous cartilage may be taken from the septum, ear or rib. Rib cartilage may be considered when stronger structural support is needed, when there is limited septal or ear cartilage, or in selected revision cases. Rib grafting involves a donor-site incision and its own recovery considerations.

Implants may be considered in selected patients, particularly for nasal bridge augmentation. The choice of material should be discussed carefully, including the benefits, limitations and risks of each option. These may include infection, movement, visibility, warping, resorption, extrusion, donor-site scarring or the need for future revision surgery.

## Functional nasal assessment

Rhinoplasty planning should include assessment of nasal breathing where relevant. Breathing concerns may be related to septal deviation, nasal valve weakness, turbinate enlargement, previous trauma, allergy or other nasal conditions.

Functional assessment may include:

- nasal airflow symptoms
- history of trauma or previous nasal surgery
- septal deviation
- internal or external nasal valve support
- turbinate or mucosal issues
- asymmetry of the nostrils or nasal passages
- relationship between aesthetic changes and breathing function

If significant medical nasal or sinus issues are suspected, assessment by an ear, nose and throat specialist may be appropriate. Aesthetic nasal reshaping should be planned in a way that does not compromise breathing.

## Revision rhinoplasty

Revision rhinoplasty may be considered after previous nose surgery when there are concerns such as asymmetry, implant problems, contour irregularities, persistent breathing difficulty, tip deformity, scarring or dissatisfaction with the appearance.

Revision surgery is usually more complex than primary rhinoplasty. Scar tissue, altered cartilage support, previous implants, limited tissue flexibility and patient expectations all affect what can safely and realistically be changed. Some revision cases may require cartilage grafting, including rib cartilage, to rebuild support.

## What happens during consultation?

A rhinoplasty consultation may include:

- discussion of the patient’s goals and preferred nasal appearance
- review of breathing symptoms, trauma history or previous surgery
- examination of nasal bridge, tip, nostrils, septum and facial proportions
- assessment of skin thickness, cartilage support and asymmetry
- discussion of open or closed rhinoplasty approaches where appropriate
- discussion of implant, septal cartilage, ear cartilage or rib cartilage options
- explanation of scars, swelling, recovery and possible revision needs
- review of medical history, medications, smoking status and allergies
- discussion of realistic expectations and limitations

Clinical photographs may be taken for documentation and planning. Patients may be asked to describe whether they prefer a subtle refinement or a more noticeable change. The consultation should also address what may not be achievable safely.

## Recovery after rhinoplasty

Recovery varies according to the procedure performed and the patient’s healing response. Swelling and bruising are common after rhinoplasty, especially around the nose and eyes in the early recovery period. A nasal splint, dressings or internal supports may be used depending on the operation.

Many patients are presentable after the early bruising and swelling settle, but nasal swelling can continue to improve over months. Tip swelling, firmness and subtle changes may take longer to settle, especially in patients with thicker skin or more extensive structural work.

Patients may be advised to avoid strenuous activity, nose trauma, pressure from glasses, nose blowing or certain sleeping positions for a period of time. The exact instructions depend on the procedure and surgeon’s assessment.

## Risks and limitations

All surgery carries risks. Potential risks of rhinoplasty may include:

- bleeding or haematoma
- infection
- swelling and bruising
- scarring
- numbness or altered sensation
- asymmetry
- contour irregularities
- breathing difficulty or persistent nasal obstruction
- septal perforation or nasal valve problems
- implant-related problems where implants are used
- cartilage graft warping, visibility, resorption or donor-site symptoms
- unsatisfactory shape or need for revision surgery
- anaesthetic risks

Rhinoplasty can refine nasal shape and facial balance, but it cannot make the nose perfectly symmetrical or ensure a specific appearance. Skin thickness, cartilage strength, facial asymmetry, healing response and scar tissue can affect the final result.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is Asian rhinoplasty different from other types of rhinoplasty?

Asian rhinoplasty often involves different anatomical considerations, including nasal bridge height, tip support, skin thickness and nostril shape. The surgical plan should be tailored to the patient’s face and goals rather than based on a single template.

### Do I need rib cartilage for rhinoplasty?

Not every patient needs rib cartilage. Rib cartilage may be considered when stronger support is needed, when other cartilage sources are limited, or in selected revision cases. The need for rib grafting depends on anatomy, previous surgery and the planned changes.

### Can rhinoplasty improve breathing?

Rhinoplasty may improve breathing in selected patients when structural obstruction is addressed. However, not all breathing problems are caused by nasal structure alone. Allergy, sinus issues, turbinate enlargement or other conditions may also need assessment.

### How long does swelling last after rhinoplasty?

Early swelling and bruising usually improve over the first few weeks, but nasal swelling can continue to settle over months. Tip swelling and subtle definition may take longer, especially after structural rhinoplasty or in patients with thicker skin.

### Is revision rhinoplasty possible?

Revision rhinoplasty may be possible in selected patients, but it is usually more complex than first-time surgery. Scar tissue, previous implants or grafts, cartilage support and skin quality affect what can safely be changed.

### Will the result look natural?

The goal is usually to create a nose that suits the patient’s facial proportions and preferences. Naturalness depends on planning, anatomy, tissue handling, healing and realistic expectations. A consultation is needed to discuss what may be achievable for the individual patient.
