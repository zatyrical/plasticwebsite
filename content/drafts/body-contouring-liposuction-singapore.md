# Body Contouring and Liposuction in Singapore

Body contouring refers to a group of procedures that reshape selected areas of the body by addressing localised fat, loose skin, tissue laxity or contour irregularities. It may include liposuction, abdominoplasty, arm lift, thigh lift, body lift or other procedures depending on the patient’s anatomy and goals.

Liposuction removes selected deposits of fat through small access points using cannulas. It is not a weight-loss procedure. It may be considered when a patient has localised fat deposits that persist despite stable weight, exercise and lifestyle measures. When there is significant loose skin, stretch marks, muscle separation or tissue laxity, liposuction alone may not be enough, and skin-removal or tightening procedures may need to be discussed.

## What is body contouring?

Body contouring surgery aims to improve the shape and proportion of specific body areas. It may address:

- localised fat deposits
- loose or hanging skin
- stretched tissue after pregnancy or weight change
- abdominal muscle separation
- contour irregularities after previous surgery or liposuction
- excess skin after significant weight loss
- body areas that are disproportionate to the patient’s frame

The appropriate plan depends on the patient’s weight stability, skin quality, fat distribution, scars, medical history, smoking status, pregnancy history, previous operations and personal priorities.

Body contouring is not a substitute for weight management. In general, patients should be close to a stable weight before surgery. Large weight changes after surgery can affect the final contour.

## Who may consider body contouring or liposuction?

Patients may consider consultation if they have concerns such as:

- persistent localised fat despite stable lifestyle measures
- abdominal skin laxity after pregnancy or weight loss
- a lower abdominal overhang or skin fold
- contour changes after previous surgery
- loose skin of the arms, thighs, trunk or back
- disproportionate fat distribution affecting clothing fit
- functional symptoms from excess skin, such as skin irritation or hygiene difficulty

Not every patient is suitable for surgery. Some patients may be advised to delay surgery until weight is stable, medical conditions are optimised, smoking has stopped, or future pregnancy plans have been considered.

## Liposuction

Liposuction is used to reduce selected areas of subcutaneous fat. Commonly treated areas may include the abdomen, flanks, thighs, arms, back, chest, neck or other areas depending on the patient’s anatomy.

The procedure usually involves:

- marking the treatment areas
- injecting fluid to help reduce bleeding and assist fat removal
- using cannulas through small access points to remove fat
- contouring gradually to reduce irregularities
- wearing compression garments after surgery

Liposuction works more predictably when skin elasticity is reasonable. If the skin is loose or stretched, removing fat may leave residual laxity. In these situations, procedures that remove excess skin may be more appropriate or may be combined with liposuction.

## Abdominoplasty and tummy tuck surgery

Abdominoplasty, commonly called a tummy tuck, addresses excess abdominal skin and tissue. It may also involve repair of rectus diastasis, where the paired abdominal muscles have separated, commonly after pregnancy or weight fluctuation.

An abdominoplasty may be considered when there is:

- loose lower abdominal skin
- an abdominal overhang
- stretch-marked skin below the umbilicus
- abdominal wall laxity or muscle separation
- contour concerns after pregnancy or weight loss
- previous scars or tissue changes that need surgical planning

Abdominoplasty involves a longer incision than liposuction. The position and length of the scar depend on the amount of skin to be removed, the patient’s anatomy and the operative plan. Scar appearance changes over time but may remain visible.

Liposuction may be combined with abdominoplasty in selected patients, but the plan must balance contour improvement with blood supply, wound healing and safety.

## Body contouring after weight loss

After major weight loss, some patients are left with excess skin that does not shrink fully. This can affect the abdomen, arms, thighs, chest, back, buttocks or other areas. Concerns may be cosmetic, functional or both.

Post-weight-loss body contouring may involve:

- abdominoplasty
- lower body lift
- arm lift
- thigh lift
- chest or breast contouring
- back or flank contouring
- staged procedures rather than a single operation

A staged approach may be safer for larger-volume surgery or when several areas need treatment. The sequence is planned according to the patient’s priorities, anaesthetic safety, wound-healing considerations and recovery capacity.

Patients who have had bariatric surgery or major weight loss may need assessment of nutrition, blood counts, protein intake, vitamin status and medical stability before body contouring.

## Arm lift and thigh lift

Arm lift and thigh lift procedures remove excess skin and tissue from the upper arms or thighs. These operations may be considered when loose skin causes discomfort, clothing difficulty, skin irritation or significant contour concern.

The trade-off is a longer scar. The position and length of the scar depend on the area treated and the amount of skin to be removed. During consultation, the likely scar position, visibility, recovery and limitations should be discussed carefully.

## Consultation and assessment

A body contouring consultation usually includes:

- discussion of goals and priorities
- review of medical history and medications
- assessment of weight stability
- examination of skin quality and tissue laxity
- assessment of fat distribution and contour balance
- review of previous scars or operations
- discussion of pregnancy plans where relevant
- explanation of surgical and non-surgical options
- discussion of recovery, risks and limitations

For patients considering larger procedures, medical optimisation may be needed before surgery. This may include blood tests, anaesthetic assessment, review of chronic medical conditions, smoking cessation and planning for time away from work or caregiving duties.

## Planning a safe and realistic procedure

A body contouring plan should be individualised. The most appropriate option depends on whether the main issue is fat, skin excess, muscle separation, tissue laxity or a combination.

Examples include:

- liposuction alone for selected localised fat deposits with reasonable skin elasticity
- abdominoplasty for abdominal skin excess and muscle separation
- combined liposuction and skin excision in selected patients
- staged surgery after major weight loss
- revision or contour-correction procedures after previous surgery

Surgery should not aim to create a standardised body shape. The goal is to improve proportion and contour in a way that is suitable for the patient’s anatomy, health, lifestyle and recovery needs.

## Recovery after body contouring surgery

Recovery depends on the procedure performed, the number of areas treated and the patient’s healing response. Liposuction recovery is usually different from recovery after abdominoplasty or larger skin-removal procedures.

After liposuction, patients commonly experience swelling, bruising, soreness and temporary numbness. Compression garments may be recommended. Many patients can return to light daily activities before all swelling has settled, but strenuous exercise usually needs to wait until medically cleared.

After abdominoplasty or larger body contouring procedures, recovery is more substantial. Patients may need to walk slightly flexed initially, manage drains if used, avoid heavy lifting and take more time away from work or exercise. If abdominal muscle repair is performed, activity restrictions may be longer.

Swelling can take weeks to months to improve. Final contour continues to change as swelling settles and tissues soften. Recovery timelines vary between patients and should be discussed during consultation.

## Risks and limitations

All surgery has risks. Body contouring and liposuction may involve risks such as:

- bleeding or haematoma
- infection
- delayed wound healing
- fluid collection or seroma
- contour irregularity or asymmetry
- skin laxity after fat removal
- numbness or altered sensation
- visible or widened scars
- skin loss or wound breakdown
- deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism
- anaesthetic risks
- need for revision surgery

Liposuction has limits. Removing too much fat or treating unsuitable tissue can increase the risk of contour problems, skin laxity or healing issues. Abdominoplasty and body lifts can remove loose skin, but they create scars and require a longer recovery.

Outcomes are influenced by anatomy, skin quality, weight stability, healing response, medical conditions, smoking status and postoperative care.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is liposuction a weight-loss procedure?

No. Liposuction is intended for body contouring rather than weight loss. It removes selected areas of fat but does not replace weight management, exercise or treatment of obesity-related health concerns.

### Can liposuction tighten loose skin?

Liposuction removes fat but does not reliably tighten loose skin. Some skin contraction may occur, but if skin laxity is significant, a skin-removal procedure may be needed to address the excess.

### What is the difference between liposuction and abdominoplasty?

Liposuction removes selected fat deposits through small access points. Abdominoplasty removes excess abdominal skin and may repair abdominal muscle separation. Some patients need one procedure, while others may need a combination or a staged plan.

### How long does swelling last after liposuction?

Swelling usually improves gradually over weeks, but subtle swelling and contour changes may continue for months. The timeline depends on the area treated, extent of surgery, compression, activity and individual healing.

### When can I exercise after body contouring surgery?

This depends on the procedure. Light walking is often encouraged early, but strenuous exercise, heavy lifting and core work may need to be delayed, especially after abdominoplasty or muscle repair. Clearance should be individualised by the treating surgeon.

### Can body contouring be done after pregnancy?

Some patients consider body contouring after pregnancy, especially if there is loose abdominal skin or muscle separation. Surgery is usually considered when recovery from pregnancy is complete, weight is stable and future pregnancy plans have been discussed.

### Can multiple areas be treated in one operation?

Sometimes, but not always. Combining procedures must be balanced against anaesthetic duration, blood loss, wound-healing risk, recovery burden and safety. Staging may be more appropriate for larger or more complex plans.

### Will scars be visible?

Skin-removal procedures such as abdominoplasty, arm lift, thigh lift or body lift create scars. Scars usually change over time but may remain visible. Scar position, length and likely visibility should be discussed before surgery.
